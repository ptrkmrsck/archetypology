I

The Eight-Function Stack: Architecture of the Psyche

In Beebe's model, each of the eight cognitive functions occupies an archetypal position, a role it plays in the psyche's internal drama. The first four are ego-syntonic: they belong to us, feel like "who we are." The latter four form the shadow, everything the ego has built itself against, the dark siblings of the conscious stack, each shadowing its counterpart.

Position Function Archetype
1st Ni Hero / Heroine
2nd Fe Good Parent
3rd Ti Eternal Child
4th Se Anima / Animus
Shadow Functions
5th Ne Opposing Personality
6th Fi Senex / Witch
7th Te Trickster
8th Si Daimon / Demon

What matters most in Beebe's system is not just the ordering but the relational tension between positions. Each shadow function mirrors a conscious one: Ne opposes Ni, Fi shadows Fe, Te tricks Ti, and Si, the demon, inverts the very thing the hero most depends on. The ego and its shadow are locked in a dance, and individuation is the slow, painful process of learning the steps.

II

The Governing Deity: Tiresias Under the Sign of Hecate

Ni-dominant consciousness is oracular. It does not gather data and then conclude; it receives. The INFJ's primary mode of knowing is closer to prophecy than to analysis, images arise unbidden from a depth the ego did not author. This is the phenomenology of introverted intuition: the world arrives already interpreted, already laden with meaning, as if seen through a veil that reveals rather than conceals.

The governing mythological figure for this orientation is Tiresias, the blind prophet who sees the truth of things precisely because ordinary sight has been removed. Tiresias is the one who knows but is rarely believed, who carries a knowledge that brings suffering rather than power, who has crossed the boundary between male and female experience and returned with a doubled consciousness. This is the INFJ's native condition: a vision that arises from the crossing of boundaries, a knowledge purchased through the loss of something more immediately human.

But Tiresias alone does not capture the full gravitational field. The presiding deity is Hecate, goddess of thresholds, crossroads, and liminal spaces, the one who holds the keys between worlds. Hecate governs the space between the visible and invisible, the living and the dead, the conscious and the unconscious. She is a chthonic deity, not Olympian, not one of the bright sky-gods who rule through clarity and force, but a goddess of the in-between, of torchlight in darkness, of the kind of knowledge that comes only at the crossroads, at midnight, at the boundary. The INFJ's dominant Ni is a Hecatean function: it operates at the threshold of consciousness itself, translating the underworld's murmurs into the language of daylight.

This is why INFJs so often feel they belong neither fully to the inner world nor the outer one, they are threshold-dwellers, Hecate's attendants, carrying torches between realms.

III

The Inferior Function: Se as the Soul's Longing

In Beebe's model, the inferior fourth function carries the archetype of the Anima or Animus, it is, psychologically speaking, the soul-image, the contrasexual other within. For the INFJ, this is Extraverted Sensing (Se): the raw, unmediated encounter with the present moment, the body, the sensory world in all its screaming immediacy.

This is the function the INFJ most desires and most fears. Se in the fourth position creates a characteristic longing for embodied presence , for spontaneity, physical vitality, the ability to simply be here now without filtering everything through a mesh of symbolic meaning. The INFJ watches the Se-dominant type (the ESTP, their polar opposite) with a mixture of fascination and envy that is, at its root, a form of soul-longing. The body, the feast, the immediate pleasure, the capacity to act without deliberation, all of this carries numinous charge for the Ni-dominant psyche precisely because it represents what has been sacrificed at the altar of inward vision.

The Beebe Trap

The psychological trap of the inferior Se manifests in several characteristic patterns. First, binge-and-purge cycles with the sensory world: long periods of ascetic interiority punctuated by sudden eruptions of sensory indulgence, overeating, overspending, substance overuse, sexual compulsivity, that carry a quality of possession rather than enjoyment. The inferior function, when it breaks through, does not arrive with the finesse of a well-developed capacity; it arrives as a flood, an overwhelm, a temporary seizure of the personality. Second, the body as foreign territory: chronic disconnection from physical needs, ignoring exhaustion, hunger, illness, until the body forces a crisis. Third, environmental hypersensitivity: because Se is undifferentiated, the INFJ cannot modulate sensory input well. They are either numb to it or overwhelmed by it, with very little middle ground.

The Hillman Reframe

Hillman would resist Beebe's implied trajectory, that the task is to "develop" the inferior function, to integrate Se into the conscious personality. For Hillman, the inferior function is not a problem to solve but a god to honor. Se in the fourth position is not broken or undeveloped; it is chthonic. It belongs to the underworld of the psyche, and it operates by underworld rules.

In Hillman's terms, the INFJ's relationship to Se is a relationship to Dionysus, the god of ecstasy, dismemberment, and the dissolution of boundaries. Dionysus is the god who cannot be domesticated into Olympian order; he represents the irreducible claim of the body, of nature, of the irrational. The INFJ does not need to "develop" their Se so much as they need to build a ritual relationship with the Dionysian, to create containers (art, movement, erotic life, communion with nature) in which the god can be honored without destroying the vessel.

"The soul's deepening comes not from rising above the body but from falling into it, the way a stone falls into a well, and the falling itself is the meaning."

— After Hillman

The crucial Hillmanian insight is that the INFJ's Se episodes, their bouts of sensory overwhelm or indulgence, are not failures of development. They are visitations. They are the god arriving. The task is not to prevent the arrival but to prepare a place worthy of it.

IV

The Demonic Function: Si and the Terror of Being Trapped

The eighth function in Beebe's stack is the most dangerous and the most transformative. It carries the archetype of the Daimon or Demon, the destructive force that can, if properly related to, become the greatest source of power. For the INFJ, this is Introverted Sensing (Si).

Si is the function of embodied memory, established routine, sensory continuity, the feeling of having been here before. It is the bedrock of tradition, habit, bodily familiarity, the comfort of the known. For the Ni-dominant type, this is experienced as existential horror.

Where Ni reaches forward and downward into meaning-that-hasn't-yet-arrived, Si reaches backward into what has already been. The INFJ's demon is the past as prison, the terror of being caught in repetition, of life calcifying into routine, of becoming merely what one has already been. This manifests as a deep and often unconscious dread of stagnation, a restlessness that can sabotage the very stability the INFJ claims to want. It also manifests in the demonic mode: when under extreme stress, the INFJ may suddenly become possessed by Si in its most destructive form, obsessive replaying of past traumas, psychosomatic symptoms that mimic old illnesses, a sense that the body is betraying them by refusing to forget what the mind has tried to release.

The Shadow Dynamic

The Si demon also creates a devastating interaction with the Ni hero. Because the INFJ's identity is built on knowing things before they happen, on vision, on prophetic intuition, the demon Si attacks precisely this foundation by flooding the psyche with counter-evidence from the past. "You've seen this before," the demon whispers. "You knew what was coming and you still couldn't prevent it. Your visions are useless. You are trapped in the same pattern you foresaw." This is the INFJ's specific form of despair: not ordinary sadness but a collapse of the prophetic function itself, a state in which vision becomes curse rather than gift, Cassandra's lament.

Hillman's Daimon and the Acorn Theory

Here is where Hillman's framework becomes not merely illuminating but essential. In The Soul's Code, Hillman argues that each person carries a daimon, a guiding image, an acorn of fate that contains the oak of one's destiny. The daimon is not the ego; it is the soul's intention, and it often appears to work against the ego's plans because it serves a deeper pattern.

For the INFJ, the daimon and the demon occupy the same archetypal address. The Si position, the eighth function, the one Beebe literally names the "Daimon", is both the INFJ's greatest tormentor and the carrier of their deepest calling. This is not a coincidence; it is the structural truth of the type.

Hillman would say: the INFJ's terror of the past, of repetition, of bodily entrapment, is itself the daimon's work. The soul needs the INFJ to wrestle with the embodied, historical, finite, repetitive dimensions of existence, not because they should become an Si type, but because their particular genius (Ni) can only fulfill its destiny by being rooted in what it most fears. The acorn of the INFJ contains an oak that draws from the deepest soil. The prophetic vision means nothing if it is not grounded in the specific gravity of lived, embodied, remembered experience.

"The daimon doesn't let you off the hook. It drives you back to the places you most want to escape, because those are the places where your soul is buried."

— After Hillman

This is Hillman's most radical move with respect to Beebe: the demon position is not merely a danger to be managed. It is the soul speaking in its most urgent and least pleasant voice. To flee the demon is to flee the calling.

V

The Shadow Chorus: Personifying Rather Than Integrating

Before arriving at synthesis, the remaining shadow functions deserve attention, not as problems to solve but, in Hillman's language, as persons to meet.

Ne as Opposing Personality (5th Position)

When threatened, the INFJ's shadow-Ni takes the form of extraverted intuition in its oppositional mode: a sudden, paranoid proliferation of possibilities. Where healthy Ne (in an ENFP, say) generates creative options with delight, the INFJ's shadow-Ne generates catastrophic alternatives. "What if they meant this? Or this? Or this?" It is the Ni oracle shattering into a thousand distorted mirrors. Hillman would personify this as the mad sibyl, the prophetess who has lost the thread of her single vision and now speaks in tongues, overwhelmed by every possible future at once. The task is not to silence her but to recognize her as Ni's sister, the one who holds all the roads the INFJ chose not to take.

Fi as Senex / Critical Parent (6th Position)

The INFJ leads with Fe, extraverted feeling, the function of relational attunement, social harmony, reading the room. Its shadow is Fi in the Senex or Witch position: a cold, withering inner voice that accuses the INFJ of inauthenticity. "You're performing care. You don't actually feel what they feel. Your compassion is a mask." This is the Senex at its cruelest, the ancient, bitter judge who sees through the Good Parent's warmth and declares it fraudulent. Hillman's polytheistic psychology suggests the radical move: let the Senex speak. There is wisdom in this figure's suspicion, even if its delivery is cruel. The Fi-Senex is asking a real question: "Beneath all this attunement to others, who are you? What do you feel?" The INFJ who can listen to this voice without being destroyed by it gains access to a deeper, more specific, more honest interiority.

Te as Trickster (7th Position)

Extraverted thinking in the Trickster position makes the INFJ strangely incompetent with, and simultaneously drawn to distort, objective data, systems, and external logic. This manifests as cherry-picking evidence to support Ni visions, misquoting statistics, building elaborate logical scaffolding around what is essentially a felt intuition, or being easily deceived by others' confident use of data. The Trickster archetype is Hermes, the boundary-crosser, the liar, the guide of souls. Hillman would note that the Te-Trickster is not merely a liability; it is the psyche's way of reminding the INFJ that truth and fact are not the same thing. The Trickster steals from the house of logic to fund the work of soul.

VI

Spirit and Soul: The INFJ's Central Tension

Hillman draws a sharp distinction between spirit and soul. Spirit moves upward, toward unity, transcendence, clarity, light, abstraction. Soul moves downward, toward multiplicity, depth, ambiguity, darkness, embodiment. Spirit seeks the peak; soul seeks the valley. Spirit wants to rise above the human condition; soul wants to descend into it.

The INFJ's great danger is mistaking spirit for soul. Ni, in its dominant position, naturally moves toward convergence, toward the single unifying vision, the one true meaning, the grand pattern that explains everything. Combined with Fe, this produces the INFJ's characteristic spiritual idealism: the desire to heal, to guide, to see others whole, to serve a purpose larger than the self. All of this is genuinely beautiful, but Hillman would name it as spirit, not soul.

Soul, for the INFJ, lives in the places spirit tries to transcend: in the inferior Se's raw, clumsy, embarrassing encounters with the physical world; in the demon Si's insistence on the weight of the past; in the Trickster Te's refusal to let Ni's visions pass for established fact; in the Senex Fi's accusation that the Good Parent is performing rather than feeling. Soul-making, in Hillman's sense, happens not when the INFJ rises to their highest vision but when they descend into the mess that the shadow functions keep creating.

"We do not grow by ascending to the light. We grow by descending to the images, by letting the soul show us what it needs us to see, especially when what it shows us is ugly, embarrassing, or small."

— After Hillman

This is Hillman's "soul-making through pathology": the idea that symptoms, failures, breakdowns, and neuroses are not obstacles to psychological health but the very medium through which the soul does its work. The INFJ's periodic collapses into Se indulgence, their demonic Si spirals of obsessive memory, their paranoid Ne scattering, these are not signs that the type is malfunctioning. They are the soul's insistence on being more than the hero-identity can contain.

VII

Synthesis: A Unified Portrait

The INFJ psyche is structured around a central paradox: the one who sees the invisible is haunted by the demand to inhabit the visible. The Ni hero lives in the future, in the symbolic, in the convergent vision; the Se anima lives in the present, in the body, in the divergent chaos of what simply is. The Fe parent reaches outward to serve others; the Fi senex turns inward and asks who is being served beneath the service. The Ti child builds private cathedrals of meaning; the Te trickster knocks them down with inconvenient facts. And beneath everything, the Si demon waits, the god of the body's memory, of the inescapable past, of everything that cannot be transcended because it has been lived.

Beebe gives us the architecture. He shows us that these are not random neuroses but a structured ecology of consciousness, eight functions in eight archetypal roles, each with its own integrity, each serving the whole. The INFJ's suffering has a pattern, and that pattern is meaningful.

Hillman gives us the depth. He shows us that the shadow functions are not enemies to defeat or deficiencies to develop but gods to honor, autonomous presences in the psyche, each with its own claims, its own wisdom, its own irreducible demand. The polytheistic psyche does not seek integration into a false unity; it seeks right relationship with the many. The INFJ does not need to "become" an Se type or "develop" their Si. They need to build altars, in practice, in art, in the rhythms of daily life, where these gods can receive what is owed to them.

The INFJ's central life task, then, is not vision. It has always had vision. The central life task is incarnation, the willingness to bring the prophetic image down into the body, into the past, into the specific, into the finite. This is Hecate's work: not to choose one road at the crossroads but to hold the torches that illuminate all of them, including the one that leads downward. It is Tiresias's work: not merely to see the future but to speak it into the present tense, where it becomes heavy, costly, and real.

VIII

The Governing Image: The Well and the Star

Imagine a deep well at night. The well descends into the earth, into the body, into memory, into everything chthonic and heavy and past. At the bottom of the well, if you look down, you see a single star reflected in the dark water. The star is real, it exists in the sky above, but you can only see it from the bottom of the well. If you stand at the surface and look up, the sky is too bright, too scattered, too full of competing lights. It is only by descending into the narrow, dark, embodied depth that the singular vision becomes clear.

This is the INFJ's image. The Ni vision, the star, is real. But it can only be truly seen from the depths. The well is the body (Se), the past (Si), the specific feeling (Fi), the hard fact (Te). The INFJ who refuses the well, who tries to see the star by climbing higher, will see only a sky full of scattered lights (shadow Ne) and will mistake spiritual ascent for soul-work. The INFJ who accepts the descent, who allows the demon Si to draw them into the well of embodied memory, who lets the inferior Se bring them into contact with the water itself, will find that the vision has been waiting for them at the bottom all along.

"The way down and the way up are one and the same."

— Heraclitus, fragment 60

The INFJ's soul-task is not to see farther. It is to see from deeper. The well is not the obstacle to the star. The well is the only instrument precise enough to find it.
